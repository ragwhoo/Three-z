The Legend Begins

In 1978, Mazda unleashed something extraordinary — the RX-7. With its lightweight body and revolutionary Wankel rotary engine, it wasn't just another sports car. It was a statement.

The perfect 50:50 weight distribution, the pop-up headlights, and that unmistakable rotary whine — this was a machine that captured hearts from the very first drive.

Engineering

The Rotary Engine

At its core, the RX-7 housed a 1.3-liter twin-rotor engine — small in displacement but immense in spirit. While others chased cubic inches, Mazda perfected the art of the spin.

No pistons. No valves. Just pure, seamless power delivered in a way no other engine could match. Smooth, high-revving, and utterly addictive.

Philosophy

Design Philosophy

Every line of the RX-7 served a purpose. The wedge-shaped profile wasn't just beautiful — it was a masterclass in aerodynamics. Pop-up headlights that defined an era. A silhouette that still stops traffic today.

Kodo design before Kodo existed. Form and function in perfect harmony.

Three Generations

SA/FB 1978–1985 The original. Light, sharp, pure.
FC 1985–1991 Turbocharged sophistication.
FD 1991–2002 The masterpiece. Twin-turbo icon.
Pop Culture

Pop Culture Icon

From the streets of Tokyo to the big screen, the RX-7 became legendary. Its starring role in Initial D made it a household name, and its presence in racing circuits around the world proved it was more than just a pretty face.

In the hands of Keisuke Takahashi, the RX-7 FD became an icon of an entire generation.

The Legacy Lives On

Though production ended in 2002, the RX-7's spirit has never faded. It remains one of the most beloved Japanese sports cars ever created, with a passionate community keeping the rotary flame alive.

The RX-7 is more than a car. It's a feeling.
